🎯 What is Quality?

Defining Quality

Quality is multifaceted and can be defined in several ways:

  • Based on judgments by an individual or organization
  • Fitness for purpose
  • Corresponds to a rating
  • Based on acceptable performance
  • Meeting goals/requirements

Quality in Software

Quality is the ability of your product to satisfy your users.

Without quality, you may have little to offer.

Quality helps to future-proof projects.

Example: Quality in Different Contexts

Banking Software: Quality means accuracy, security, and reliability in financial transactions.

Mobile Game: Quality means engaging gameplay, smooth performance, and intuitive controls.

Medical System: Quality means precision, data integrity, and regulatory compliance.

🛡️ Quality Assurance

What is Quality Assurance?

Quality Assurance is the process that demonstrates your product is able to satisfy your users.

When good quality assurance is implemented there should be:

  • Improvements in usability and performance
  • Reduction in defect rates

Key Aspects of QA

Quality Assurance needs documented standards and best practices to be meaningful.

Quality & Best Practice can be considered in terms of being Fit for Purpose.

Establish Environment
Define Processes
Measure Effectiveness
Continuous Improvement

💡 Remember:

QA focuses on prevention rather than detection. It's about creating systems that make it hard to introduce defects in the first place.

🔍 Quality Control

What is Quality Control?

Quality Control (QC) is the implementation of regular testing procedures against your definitions of quality and more specifically the refinement of these procedures.

QC Characteristics

  • Formal use of testing
  • Acting on the results of your tests
  • Requires planning, structured tests, good documentation
  • Relates to output - Quality Circle
  • Standards compliance
Aspect Quality Assurance (QA) Quality Control (QC) Focus Prevention of defects Detection of defects Approach Process-oriented Product-oriented Activities Process definition, standards, training Testing, inspections, reviews

Real-world Example

QA: Implementing coding standards, conducting training sessions, setting up code review processes.

QC: Executing unit tests, performing integration testing, conducting user acceptance testing.

🌳 McCall's Factor Model

About McCall's Model

A quality factor represents a behavioral characteristic of the system.

A quality criterion is an attribute of a quality factor that is related to software production and design.

A quality metric is a measure that captures some aspect of a quality criterion.

💡 Historical Note:

McCall's model was developed in 1977 by J.A. McCall, P.K. Richards, and G.F. Walters in their work "Factors in Software Quality". It remains influential in software quality assessment today.

🧪 Types of Testing

Based on SDLC Stages

  • Unit Testing: Individual components
  • Integration Testing: Groups of components
  • System Testing: Entire system
  • Acceptance Testing: By client/users

Based on Testing Approach

  • Static Testing:
    • Hand execution: Reading the source code
    • Walk-Throughs
    • Code Inspections
    • Automated Tools checking
  • Dynamic Testing:
    • Black-box testing (input/output behavior)
    • White-box testing (internal logic)

Acceptance Test Driven Development (ATDD)

ATDD is an agile system development approach that serves as a communication process between the customer, developer & tester.

It validates 'Building the Right Code' through executable specifications that are meaningful to business, developers & testers.

Why We Need ATDD - The Elephant Story

Customer Requirements:

  1. Have one trunk
  2. Have four legs
  3. Should carry load both passenger & cargo
  4. Black in color
  5. Should be herbivorous

Our Solution: A black cow that meets all requirements and also gives milk!

This illustrates how misunderstandings can lead to incorrect solutions without proper acceptance criteria.

⚠️ Dealing with Errors and Faults

What is an Error?

An erroneous state ("error") occurs when something goes wrong in the system.

Before we can identify an error, we need to specify the desired behavior first!

Types of Faults

  • Algorithmic Fault: Errors in the logic or processing
  • Mechanical Fault: Hardware or physical failures

Approaches to Deal with Errors

  • Verification:
    • Assumes environment and checks with specification
    • May omit important constraints
  • Modular redundancy: Expensive but reliable
  • Declaring a bug as a "feature": Bad practice
  • Patching: Can slow down performance
  • Testing: Never good enough alone

Comprehensive Error Management

  1. Error prevention (before release):
    • Good programming methodology
    • Version control
    • Verification
  2. Error detection (while running):
    • Testing
    • Debugging
    • Monitoring
  3. Error recovery (after failure):
    • Atomic transactions
    • Modular redundancy
    • Recovery blocks

💡 Key Observations:

  • It is impossible to completely test any module or any system
  • Testing can only show the presence of bugs, not their absence (Dijkstra)
  • Testing requires creativity and independent perspective

🧠 Practice Quiz

Test Your Understanding:

Q1: What is the main difference between Quality Assurance and Quality Control?
Answer: QA focuses on preventing defects through process improvement (process-oriented), while QC focuses on detecting defects through testing and inspection (product-oriented).
Q2: Name three categories in McCall's Factor Model and give one example from each.
Answer:
  • Product Operation: Reliability (system works consistently)
  • Product Revision: Maintainability (easy to modify)
  • Product Transition: Portability (works across environments)
Q3: What are the three main approaches to dealing with errors in software systems?
Answer:
  1. Error prevention (before release)
  2. Error detection (while running)
  3. Error recovery (after failure)
Q4: Why is Acceptance Test Driven Development (ATDD) important?
Answer: ATDD helps prevent misunderstandings between customers, developers and testers by creating executable specifications that serve as common ground. It validates that we're "building the right code" by focusing on user needs from the beginning.
Q5: What makes testing a creative activity rather than just a mechanical process?
Answer: Testing requires understanding the system deeply, knowing various testing techniques, and skillfully applying them. Effective testers need to think beyond obvious scenarios, anticipate edge cases, and design tests that reveal hidden problems. It's about asking "how could this break?" rather than just "does this work?"
